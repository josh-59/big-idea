Welcome

Dear Reader,

The goal of this website is to describe, in gory detail, the operation of the mind. The principle vehicle for this is the process, a function without fixed points. Broadly speaking, the pattern about the deductive mind is to observe, reason, then conclude. I believe that each of these steps can be framed in terms of simplification, formally, abstraction. Through this website, those parts will be broken down and examined, and suitable formal theory will be built up.

Published here are ideas of my own. I've been working on these ideas for some time, and would like now to put them into the public domain. The material begins with the assumptions of the field of First Order Logic, namely, the existence of well-defined "objects," in addition to some elementary theory of sets and functions.

The theory revolves around two new ideas: Processes, a kind of function; and, Abstraction, the process of simplification. Although I've done my best, I wish to consider each essay to be up for review; the purpose of the website is to motivate me to complete the exposition of these ideas, and to improve them through the criticism of others. Enjoy!

       – Josh

Thursday, May 28, 2020: A recap: On the one hand, it's very nice to have the operation of abstraction formally defined (namely, taking a subset). That operation seems to accurately reflect everything I know about abstractions, in a more precise manner; it "bodes" well with everyday use, and, simultaneously, it allows more precise thinking about them. In particular, it works well when applied to, say, the set of features that an object has.

However, we require a substantial, non-contradictory definition of abstractions within some greater mathematical "language", to make further progress. I'm loathe to give any part of this development up, but, some concession must be made. The least concession would be to allow a null process; that is, to discard processes in favor of functions. It's a little less pretentious, as well.
