Abstraction in Action

An Example from Graph Theory

Our second example is Leonard Euler's famous solution to the Köonigsberg bridges problem. The following passage is from John V Guttag's Intoduction to Computation and Programming Using Python, second edition, reprinted courtesy of the MIT Press:

"Graphs are typically used to represent situations in which there are interesting relations among the parts. The first documented use of graphs in mathematics was in 1735 when the Swiss mathematician Leonhard Euler used what has come to be known as graph theory to formulate and solve the Königsberg bridges problem.

Köonigsberg, then the capital of East Prussia, was built at the intersection of two rivers that contained a number of islands. The islands were connected to each other and to the mainland by seven bridges, as shown on the map on the left side of Figure 12.6. For some reason, the residents of the city were obsessed with the question of whether it was possible to take a walk that crossed each bridge exactly once.

Euler's great insight was that the problem could be vastly simplified by viewing each separate landmass as a point (think "node") and each bridge as a line (think "edge") connecting two of these points. The map of the town could then be represented by the graph to the right of the map in Figure 12.6. Euler then reasoned that if a walk were to traverse each edge exactly once, it must be the case that each node in the middle of the walk (i.e., any island that is both entered and exited during the walk) must be connected by an even number of edges. Since none of the nodes in the graph has an even number of edges, Euler concluded that it is impossible to traverse each bridge exactly once."

The set of bridges and islands in this example is precisely defined, and so represents an object, in the First Order Language view of things.

The graph is an abstraction of the bridges. Clearly, we have abstracted away from the bridges; less clearly, we also abstract away from the problem itself. Euler rephrased the question in simpler terms with, "Is each island connected by an even number of bridges?" The information to answer this question is preserved in the graph, and thus the question gets rephrased as, "Is each node connected by an even number of edges?"

When we consult the graph and find that none of the nodes has an even number of edges, we arrive at the conclusion that the walk is, indeed, not possible. This conclusion represents an abstraction of our argument.

Concluding

Abstraction comes in many forms. In the previous example, the graph is an explicit abstraction of the collection of islands. That pattern of thought is found throughout much of science, and has been the subject of extensive inquiry. But we also abstract implicitly by, for instance, "assuming" the results of proofs already completed. In some sense, an argument's conclusion is an abstraction from the argument itself.

Abstracting is an important part of reasoning. For the intellect, it does a few things:

  1. Simplifies the picture to allow the intellect to focus on what is most important // How do we know that what the intellect is focusing on is important? Often, we don't! Often, the intellect is simply wrong.
  2. Preserves the most relevant information // Relevant with respect to what, exactly?
  3. Opens up "mental space" to facilitate reasoning about these things // But what is "reasoning"?
A point worth pondering: If you aren't abstracting, then what are you doing?
