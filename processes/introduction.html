Introduction

We begin our investigation of the mind with the assumption that the mind is a component of the human body, where the body is separate from the world. Formally, we will call "the mind" an intellect.

If the human body has five senses, and a brain which interprets these senses, then the above must be an accurate depiction of the overarching role of the mind.

The singular operation that will be entertained throughout this development is simplification; formally, we will call this operation abstraction. A single abstraction acts like a mathematical function, with input and output.

If the abstraction is nontrivial, then the function has distinct inputs and outputs. If the abstraction is trivial, then no change has been made; this precludes such "operations" from investigation.

Unlike a single abstraction, an intellect does not begin at one point and end at another. Rather, it exists between two connections, which are connected by something other than itself (above, that thing is denoted by "reality"). In other words, an intellect observes the results of its own output; equally, an intellect indirectly interacts with its input.

With the above constraints in mind, I will define the intellect to be a sequence of overlapping abstractions, each of which implicitly changes the intellect's abstracting process.
