The Process of Abstraction

Draft

The simplest possible process: Discarding.

Definition

Suppose $X$ is a set, and let $p$ be a process on $\mathscr{P}(X)$. Then $p(X)$ is an abstracting process on $\mathscr{P}(X)$ if, for all $x\in\mathscr{P}(X), p(x)\subset x$.

Now, even as a lowly (ex-)mathematics-major, I can see that we have a problem: $\varnothing \in {\mathscr{P}(X)}$, so that $p(\varnothing)$ must be defined, yet, $\varnothing$ has no elements, so that there does not exist an element in $\varnothing$ such that $p(\varnothing) \subset \varnothing$.

For the moment, let us consider, philosophically, the ramifications of abstracting from nothing:

The question is, What would $p(\varnothing)$ be?  Whatever exists at $p(\varnothing)$ would be a consequence of the abstracting process itself, and not a result of anything that it has observed. This would be true with respect to the abstracting process.

In particular, if $p$ is a metaprocess, then $p(\varnothing)$ would be the constituent processes of $p$.
