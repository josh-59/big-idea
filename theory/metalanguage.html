Metalanguage

Initially inspired by words in Dr. Suppes's Axiomatic Set Theory, here I generalize the notion in order to provide a more formal, if still intuitive, description of learning: All learning can be expressed as adopting new metalanguage.

In the aforementioned Axiomatic Set Theory, it is explicitly pointed out (page 11) that, "Our metalanguage is a certain vaguely defined fragment of ordinary English augmented by certain symbols familiar from intuitive mathematics." Within that work, this metalangauge is used to develop the "object language" of set theory.

As yet, I have no desire to develop object languages, but I see great potential in the idea of metalangauge to help describe how the mind operates.

Intuitively, a metalanguage is the language used in the course of some work. It is a language that has been adopted by the actor as native material; it is part of themselves. Moreover, the actor uses the metalanguage to attain some goal.

As a first example, LaTeX is a metalanguage. On the one hand, it must be learned (and is not the easiest program to learn). On the other, it exists for the sole purpose of typesetting documents. One learns LaTeX, and then goes about forgetting it as quickly as possible, in order to achieve their ultimate goal of publishing some paper.

This first example is intended to illustrate the generality I seek in the development of "metalanguages." Many other programs may be considered metalanguages:

  • Word
  • Git
  • CAD
  • MATLAB
  • Video production / Music Production Suites

An interesting thought-experiment: A layman could probably pick up a LaTeX source document and work out what it says (given that all they are lacking is knowledge of LaTeX). On the other hand, it would be unreasonable to expect them to turn around and use the program to create documents of their own. That is, the metalanguage of LaTeX is spoken in the syntax and semantics of LaTeX source, and not in the English (or mathematical) content of the document.

The next interesting example comes from science. It is often said, "Chemistry is everywhere." I had never understood this expression until phrasing it in terms of metalanguages: Chemistry is the formal language of materials, and it is spoken everywhere that materials are considered (which is everywhere).

Metalanguages are subjects which are well-defined, in the sense that they have bounds, and which are internalized (effectively, assumed) for their subsequent use in the pursuit of an arbitrary goal.

A few more examples:

  • A crime fiction's metalangauge is English; its purpose is to entertain
  • Mechanics is a metalanguage of auto technicians
  • Both music itself and instruments are metalanguages of the musician

For any metalanguage, we require some layer underneath it, in order to discuss it. However, we do not need additional layers to use it. As in the case of English class, we can use a language as its own metalanguage.

The primary distinction that the metalanguage abstraction allows us to make is between working and studying:

  • Working: Using what you know
    • Picking new knowledge up incidentally
  • Studying: Reconsidering what you know
