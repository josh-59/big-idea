Introduction

We begin our investigation of the mind with the assumption that the mind is a component of the human body, where the body is separate from the world.

If the human body has five senses, and a brain which interprets these senses, then the above must be an accurate depiction of the overarching role of the mind.

The singular operation that will be entertained throughout this development is simplification; formally, we will call this operation abstraction. A single abstraction acts like a mathematical function, with input and output.

If the abstraction is nontrivial, then it has distinct inputs and outputs. If the abstraction is trivial, then no change has been made; this precludes such "operations" from investigation.

As depicted above, we will assume that the mind exists between two connections, which are separated by something other than itself— above, that thing is called "reality." That is, a mind observes the results of its own output; equally, it indirectly interacts with its input.

These observations serve to frame the rest of our inquiry. Ultimately, I hope to define the intellect as a sequence of semi-discrete abstractions, each of which implicitly changes the mind's abstracting process.
