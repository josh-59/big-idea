Intellect

Definition: An intellect is sequence of abstractions of finite, constant order.

We will refer to these as first-order intellect, second-order intellect, and so on. Intuitively, an intellect is a pattern of thinking. In particular, we do not care about the content of thought, when considering the intellect.

Conjecture: The number of repetitions required to adopt (assume) a consequence is one-half the order of the intellect.

Depth

An intellect need not be as simple as a sequence of singular abstractions. Rather, a single abstraction may be constructed as a sequence of multiple, smaller abstractions:

This we will refer to as the depth of the intellect.

Conjecture: The human mind is principally defined by an intellect with order between one and eight. Moreover, the average human intellect has a depth of four.

When speaking of the order of an intellect, we do not refer to the order of its abstractions.

Intellect As a Data Structure

One approach towards describing the intellect might be as a data structure with particular properties. Specifically, it would be a data structure which is endlessly economizing. Formally, that would be expressed as operating in $O(0)$ (Big-Oh-Zero) time.

Conjecture: Any data structure operating in O(0) time will prove to be an intellect.

This view is as-yet undeveloped, and questions remain: What is its interface? What do I mean by O(0)?

Implementation of Intellect

A few characteristics of a possible implementation of intellect:

  1. The abstracting process must be a process on the mind. That is, at each possible interaction, zero change is not possible. We stipulate this because without change, the abstracting process cannot be said to exist.

  2. The abstracting process is usually negative in the sense that for the majority of interactions, the abstracting processes discards (marginalizes) the belief.

  3. The elements have "memory," so that when they are suppressed, they tend to "bounce back."

  4. It would be possible to "subconsciously" increase the importance of an element, so that it is not necessary to entertain a thought explicitly, in order to maintain it in thought.

  5. The importance of an element is defined by its relation among other elements.

  6. The generation of a new node in this network is but a slight generalization of abductive reasoning. It would not need to be true with respect to anything except other nodes, in order to be considered important. A statement to this effect would be, "That is what I believed."
