Thought

"Our everyday use of language is vague,
and our everyday level of thinking is often muddled."
Dr. Patrick Suppes
Introduction to Logic, page xi

This portion of the development builds definitions and introduces key ideas. It does not attempt to explain why a mind would think.

Definition: A thought is a finite sequence of observations and abstractions $$(a_1, b_1), (a_2,b_2), ..., (a_n, b_n)$$ such that $b_1 = a_2, b_2 = a_3, ...$, and $(a_1, b_n)$ forms an abstraction.

With this definition, our model should become intuitively clear: We are imagining a container of information is being operated on reiteratively. The two operations are to add information and to subtract information. Each operation $(a_x, b_x)$ is defined outside of the thought, and is therefore arbitrary with respect to the thought.

Depictions such as the one above will be common in what follows, and therefore it may be well to explain them precisely. On the y-axis is data, and on the x-axis is time. Each line plotted represents a distinct container of information; any point on the line represents the quantity of information it contains at that time. Dotted lines do not represent containers of information, and they will be used only to illustrate relevant concepts. Finally, note that in the depiction above, the curve denoting the thought is composed of strictly abstractions, since the quantity of information is monotonically decreasing.

By way of thought, the mind generates new abstractions for itself: A new operation is produced, which may be included in other thoughts. Obtaining this new abstraction from a thought involves only observing the thought's container of information at two distinct points in time.
