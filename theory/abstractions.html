Abstractions

"Our everyday use of language is vague, and our everyday level of thinking is often muddled."
Dr. Patrick Suppes
Introduction to Logic, page xi

An abstraction discards information; intuitively, an abstraction is a simplification. Every abstraction acts like a mathematical function, with distinct input and output.

Definition of Abstraction

Below are a few collected definitions of abstractions.


1

"... abstraction: A simplification of something much more complicated that is going on under the covers."

Joel Spolsky, The Law of Leaky Abstractions , retrieved March 6

2

"Abstraction hides detail."

John V. Guttag, Introduction to Computation and Programming Using Python, 2nd edition, p. 49.

3

"An abstraction [sic] is a view or representation of an entity that includes only the most significant attributes."

Robert W. Sebesta, Concepts of Programming Languages 11ed, p. 448.

Definition: An abstraction is a discarding process.

Because abstractions exist in multiple contexts, we must differentiate them in a meaningful way. We will divide abstractions into two classes, pure and impure.

Pure Abstractions

Definition: A pure abstraction is an abstraction of a single entity.

In physics, kinematics is introduced by first modeling an object as a point. This point representation of the object is a pure abstraction of the object.

In computer science, the bit is a pure abstraction of underlying hardware. It is defined to have only one of two possible states, and these states may be implemented in a variety of ways.

In each of the above examples, the abstraction yields a many-to-one relationship: Many objects can be modeled as points, and many pieces of hardware can be thought of as bits. This holds in the general case: Abstraction never adds information. That is, every abstraction is more general than the thing it discards from.

Impure Abstractions

Although certainly important, pure abstractions are too clean and neat to describe the operation of the mind in the general case.

Belief and Abstraction

In common usage, the word "thought" has two distinct meanings. The first is found in the response to the question, "What are you thinking?" This equivalently asks things like, "Are you upset?", "Are you mad?", "Would you like to sit down?", etc.. The second usage is found in, "There's a thought!", or, "What a great idea!"

These are two different things, and we must separate them in order to talk about them clearly. We will use the word beliefs to speak of the first entity, and the words thought and abstraction to synonymously refer to the second entity.
