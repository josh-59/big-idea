Abstractions

An abstraction is a simplification; abstracting discards information in order to produce something simpler. A single abstraction acts like a mathematical function, with input and output.

If the abstraction is nontrivial, then it has distinct inputs and outputs. If the abstraction is trivial, then no change has been made; this precludes such "operations" from investigation.

Several fields provide good examples of abstractions.

Examples of Abstraction In Computer Science

In computer science, abstraction is extremely important, and is necessarily defined in concrete terms (in virtue of the bit). For instance, the bit itself is an abstraction: It is defined to have only one of two possible states, and these states may be implemented in a variety of ways. We discard the actual state of the physical device, and focus on its relevant state.

Within programming, a common practice is to write subroutines that carry out some small task well. This allows the programmer to break up a complex task into a sequence of simpler ones. Intrinsic to this process is the abstraction away from these subroutines: They are each individually written, tested, and ensured to work. Then, they are abstracted away from by discarding the implementing details.

Finally, I wish to say that computer science provided the genesis of this entire development— specifically, Joel Spolsky's The Law of Leaky Abstractions. To express my appreciation, and for a clear example of an abstraction, I ask that you read his essay.

Mathematics and Abstraction

Mathematical objects are entirely abstract. That is, mathematics considers the case when simplification from reality has already taken place. Objects within mathematics are well-defined abstractions.

Examples Within Logic

For a mathematics student such as myself, propositional logic is an intellectual tool. Much like a table or a diagram, it serves to aid my thinking. The usefulness of propositional logic is that it begins abstracting from the statement: I can view any mathematical statement as a proposition, and that proposition becomes an abstraction of my statement. This recasts my statement into the field of propositional logic, where substantial and relevant theory exists.

An example of reasoning by abstraction: In propositional logic, the transitive law of implication states that, given the premises, "A implies B", and "B implies C", we may conclude "A implies C." This reasoning is an act of abstraction: We are discarding statement B in its entirity.

Definition of Abstraction

Below are a few collected definitions of abstractions.


1

"... abstraction: A simplification of something much more complicated that is going on under the covers."

Joel Spolsky, The Law of Leaky Abstractions , retrieved March 6

2

"Abstraction hides detail."

John V. Guttag, Introduction to Computation and Programming Using Python, 2nd edition, p. 49.

3

"An abstraction [sic] is a view or representation of an entity that includes only the most significant attributes."

Robert W. Sebesta, Concepts of Programming Languages 11ed, p. 448.

Definition: An abstraction is a discarding process.

Belief and Abstraction

In common usage, the word "thought" has two distinct meanings. The first is found in the response to the question, "What are you thinking?" This equivalently asks things like, "Are you upset?", "Are you mad?", "Would you like to sit down?", etc.. The second usage is found in, "There's a thought!", or, "What a great idea!"

These are two different things, and we must separate them in order to talk about them clearly. We will use the word beliefs to speak of the first entity, and the words thought and abstraction to synonymously refer to the second entity.
