Abstraction

"Our everyday use of language is vague, and our everyday level of thinking is often muddled."
Dr. Patrick Suppes
Introduction to Logic, page xi

Abstraction is the act of discarding information. Intuitively, abstraction is simplification.

Definition: An abstraction is any realization of a discarding process.

While we require some data to abstract from, the accumulation of data is not our primary interest. Rather, only the discarding of data concerns us. Nonetheless, our depiction may be intuitively understood as being composed of two parts, observation and discarding:

We choose to view the abstraction as a function, for it is not the data which is our primary interest; rather, our primary interest is the process of discarding it. Concurrently, we will use the word "argument" as a synonym for "observation," and "result" as a synonym for "discarding." The instance of the function which yielded the change is our abstraction proper.

Belief and Abstraction

In common usage, the word "thought" has two distinct meanings. The first is found in the response to the question, "What are you thinking?" This equivalently asks things like, "Are you upset?", "Are you mad?", "Would you like to sit down?", etc.. The second usage is found in, "There's a thought!", or, "What a great idea!"

These are two different things, and we must separate them in order to talk about them clearly. We will use the word beliefs to speak of the first entity, and the words thought and abstraction to synonymously refer to the second entity.
