Abstraction

"All non-trivial abstractions, to some degree, are leaky."
Joel Spolsky
The Law of Leaky Abstractions

Abstractions form the heart and soul of this theory. Through them, the objects of the mind and the activity of thinking may be related in a fundamental way.

Definition: An abstraction is an ordered pair of two collections of information, $(a,b)$ such that $b$ is a proper subset of $a$.

For example, while "red" may be an abstraction from "truck", "truck" cannot be an abstraction from "red".

As a data type, an abstraction is a collection of data with structure: It contains two distinct collections of data, $a$ and $b$, with the property that $b$ is found in $a$. More, they are not layered. That is, heirarchies are a derived type.

The two fundamental operations of abstractions are to observe it and to apply it. To observe an abstraction is to observe a thought; and, to apply an abstraction is to think. In either case, we obtain a collection of information.

Definition: An abstracting function is a collection of abstractions.

An abstracting function need not be one-to-one.

In order to make this development sound, an entity complimentary to abstraction must be introduced.

Definition: An observation is a mapping from some (possibly empty) collection of information to a proper superset of it.

Definition: An idea is a collection of information that is not an ordered pair. In particular, a collection of abstractions $A$ such that $|A| > 1$ is not an ordered pair.

We may obtain an idea from an abstraction by supposing its argument and applying the abstraction. Alternatively, we may define an abstraction $i$ which maps an abstraction to an idea: $$i = ((a,b), b)$$

Definition: An oversimplification is an abstraction which discards the most relevant, pertinent or important information.

Oversimplification will not be very useful in this theory formally, but will be valuable with respect to talking about it, and so belongs "alongside" the material.
