Abstraction

"Our everyday use of language is vague,
and our everyday level of thinking is often muddled."
Dr. Patrick Suppes
Introduction to Logic, page xi

In my opinion, abstractions are fundamental to the mind: Through abstraction, the objects of the mind and the activity of thinking may be related in a fundamental way. Let's look at these each, in turn.

The first way to view an abstraction as an idea, its noun form. In this form, an abstraction is an idea with the property that it is a simplification of some other idea. This formalizes nicely: If $X$ is some collection of information, and $Y$ is a proper subset of $X$, then $Y$ is an abstraction of $X$.

The second way to view an abstraction is as an act, and to formalize the verb form of the word. This also formalizes nicely: An abstraction is a mapping from some information to less information.

Which definition is more appropriate is situation-dependant: When one is carrying out abstraction, the second defintion is more appropriate; when one is observing the result of the act, the first definition is more appropriate.

However, if we define abstraction to be a mapping from some information to less information, we get the second definition for free: since the 'mapping' will be an ordered pair.

PICTURE HERE

Owing to the nature of our pursuit, we require both definitions, and so we will associate the word simplification with one of them:

Definition: A simplification is a collection of information with the property that it is a subset of some other collection of information.

Definition: An abstraction is a mapping from some information to less information.

Notes:

  1. While simplification may be intuitively synonymous with "abstraction", they will not be considered formally synonymous.
  2. We associate "abstraction" with mapping, since we will be principally interested in abstraction as a process.

Observation and Abstraction

Observing and abstracting is a strategy for using the mind's memory; this strategy will emerge a few times in different contexts, but for now, it will be sufficient to explain only the lowest-level use that it is put to— namely, querying memory.

Here we have a data vs. time plot expressing the idea:

To query memory intelligently, the mind could first observe, and then cease observing. That part of the observation which does not change, or changes the least, is more well-known: It is remembered. That part of the observation which does change is less well-known: It is new.

The converse of this activity is "zoning out."
